Why Another Water Filter Company?

Quality drinking water is an oft-misunderstood concept. When it exists, it is unappreciated, and when it doesn’t, you panic. But in the middle of this spectrum is the not-very fine line of 2.1 billion people who lack access to safely managed drinking water services. The 340,000 annual worldwide child mortality rate from diarrhoeal diseases and the up to 5% GDP of lost productivity time in many countries”.

In our opinion, 2.1 billion is too low of an estimate as it mainly focuses on water “free from pathogens and elevated levels of harmful substances”. We believe that “safe” drinking water should not justbe in the short term but also in the long term. But taking this approach requires a fairly detailed understanding of water quality. So before we get into that, we will look briefly into current water supplies in developing countries.

Many people get their water from wells and springs in developing countries. The wealthier populations living in cities will get their water from municipally treated tap water. This problem is two fold, water from wells and springs may be contaminated through industry or agricultural run-off. Nitrites and nitrates from fertilisers are a particularly bad problem for the young and frail. While those in cities may suffer as well from leakages in aging infrastructure.

People in developing countries have taken this problem into their own hands with a combination of methods.

Boiling Water

If electrical heating is an option, then boiling is effective for the inactivation of “bacteria, viruses and protozoa”. Many cultures in South Asia have made drinking boiled water a tradition, especially when it comes with tea drinking. However, boiling water does not actually remove other harmful substances from the water, unless distilled; it just increases the concentration of these substances.

Bottled Water

Wealthier populations may use bottled water as their source of liquids. While many bottling companies are large and reputable, there is uncertainty to whether all of them conform to their national standards as enforcement may not be fully effective . On top of this, bottled water is ridiculously expensive , may have issues with microplastics and comes with extra environmental problems.

Reverse Osmosis

After all these different options, reverse osmosis (RO) point-of-use systems seem to be heralded as solution for the water drinking problem. It works by pushing water through a membrane that removes salts and other contaminants, leaving fairly pure water.

However, this also takes out the mineral content causing the water to become highly aggressive, easily dissolving materials it comes into contact with. Treatment plants would remineralise the water to stop it from leaching piping materials back into the water. But this has effects on health.

The World Health Organisation have found that higher levels of calcium, and magnesium have correlated with lower levels of cardiovascular disease. Demineralised water was found to also leach electrolytes from tissues and cause a whole range of health problems. While the WHO focuses on the role of calcium and magnesium, other important minerals are stripped away such as fluorides that have beneficial effects on dental health. The interesting thing was that effects such as "increased cardiovascular disorders, tiredness, weakness [and] muscular cramps” were reported only after a few months Czech and Slovak populations started using reverse osmosis systems in their homes in 2002. While diet could potentially provide the required nutrients, the modern diet is often deficient of the right amount of nutrients. Micronutrients found in water are also in free ion form, which are better absorbed than if it were consumed as food. While some RO POU purifiers add a calcium remineralization cartridge at the end-point, often this is only to reduce the aggressive nature of the water. Other essential minerals are not re-added.

RO membranes also faces a structural problem in that their membranes are susceptible to bacteria growth (biofouling) and prone to leakages. This requires that they need to replaced very regularly to maintain their stated water quality statistics. Not only this, but waste water is upwards of 90% with RO purifiers leading to calls for regulations on their use in India.
